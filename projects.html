Research Projects

Our research focuses on understanding the fundamental principles of protein folding and applying these insights to develop novel therapeutics for cancer and neurodegenerative disorders. Below are our current major research projects.

01

Project Title 1: Understanding Protein Folding Mechanisms

Active

Overview

This project investigates the fundamental mechanisms by which proteins fold into their native three-dimensional structures. We use advanced biophysical techniques and computational approaches to understand the energy landscapes that govern protein folding pathways.

Research Questions

  • What are the key determinants of protein folding kinetics and thermodynamics?
  • How do molecular chaperones assist in proper protein folding?
  • What causes proteins to misfold and aggregate in disease states?

Approaches & Techniques

  • Molecular dynamics simulations
  • Single-molecule fluorescence spectroscopy
  • Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy
  • Cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM)

Impact

Understanding protein folding mechanisms is crucial for developing therapeutics for protein misfolding diseases such as Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, and various cancers.

Funding: Research Grants Council (RGC)
Collaborators: Department of Chemistry, CUHK
Publications: See related publications
02

Project Title 2: Development of Protein-Based Therapeutics

Active

Overview

This project applies our understanding of protein folding to engineer novel biologics and therapeutic strategies for cancer and neurodegenerative disorders. We design and optimize protein-based drugs with enhanced stability, specificity, and therapeutic efficacy.

Research Goals

  • Design stable and effective protein-based therapeutics
  • Develop targeted delivery systems for biologics
  • Engineer proteins with enhanced pharmacological properties
  • Validate therapeutic candidates in disease models

Therapeutic Targets

  • Cancer-associated proteins and signaling pathways
  • Aggregation-prone proteins in neurodegenerative diseases
  • Immune checkpoint proteins for cancer immunotherapy

Methods

  • Rational protein design and engineering
  • High-throughput screening
  • Cell-based assays and animal models
  • Structure-guided drug design

Translational Impact

This research has the potential to yield novel therapeutic candidates that could advance to clinical development for treating currently incurable diseases.

Funding: Innovation and Technology Commission (ITC)
Collaborators: School of Pharmacy, Medical School
Status: Preclinical development phase
